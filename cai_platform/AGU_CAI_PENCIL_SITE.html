Green buildings still have people inside them

the quiet failure of certification points

Buildings earn badges by collecting points. Occupants judge them by whether they can hear, breathe, see, and feel comfortable. CAI asks one uncomfortable question: do the points follow the pain?

What we found so far

All systems lean negative

Every system/version in the current dataset has negative CAI. That means the point order is not following the occupant complaint order.

LEED is the harsh case

LEED versions analyzed range from tau = -0.913 to -1.000. The acoustic share remains tiny compared with the complaint benchmark.

WELL improves, but not enough

WELL v2 scores much better than WELL v1, but it still underweights acoustics and thermal comfort relative to occupant complaints.

How to make it paper-level

CAI becomes much stronger when it predicts real post-occupancy satisfaction. Right now, it is a certification-design alignment analysis. The next level is validation: compare CAI scores with actual satisfaction from real buildings.

Published evidence check

Extract outcomes from studies comparing LEED, WELL, and green buildings. Test whether the CAI ordering agrees with reported satisfaction patterns.

Ask for de-identified data

Email CBE / WELL / POE researchers for building-level aggregates: anonymous building ID, certification, satisfaction means, respondent count.

Campus pilot

Survey occupants in accessible certified buildings using five questions: acoustics, thermal, lighting, air, overall satisfaction.

Correlation test

Run CAI vs satisfaction. If higher CAI predicts higher satisfaction, the index becomes a validated instrument, not just a descriptive score.

Who can use this?

Future users

  • Building science researchers testing certification systems.
  • Campus sustainability teams comparing building priorities.
  • Architects checking whether design targets match occupant pain.
  • Certification bodies considering IEQ point reweighting.
  • Students building a stronger POE validation dataset.

Why it is good

It turns a vague claim into a measurable one. Instead of saying certifications care about occupants, CAI asks whether their point structures rank occupant problems in the same order occupants do.

It is transparent, reproducible, and easy to challenge. If someone has better occupant data or better credit extraction, the tool can rerun the score.
